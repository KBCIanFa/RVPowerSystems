1. Getting Started

RV Power Designer helps you size an off-grid solar power system for your RV, campervan, motorhome, or boat. The workflow is:

  1. Loads tab — Enter every appliance you plan to run and your system preferences.
  2. Sizing tab — Review the calculated battery, solar, controller, and inverter requirements.
  3. Components tab — Select products and generate a shopping list.
  4. Wiring tab — Follow the step-by-step installation guide.
  5. Save your design and compare multiple builds.
ℹ️ All calculations update live as you enter data. The live summary in the right-hand sidebar always shows your current totals.
⚠️ These results are planning estimates. Always verify sizing with a qualified solar installer before purchasing components, particularly for large or high-voltage systems.

2. System Preferences — Voltage & AC Mains

System Voltage (12V / 24V / 48V)

This is your battery bank and DC bus voltage — one of the most important decisions in your build.

VoltageBest ForWire SizeMax Practical SolarNotes
12VVans, small campers, simple weekend rigsHeaviest (high amps)~800W before upgradingMost components available, easiest to find 12V appliances
24VTouring motorhomes, medium boats, full-timersMedium~2,000WHalf the current of 12V — thinner wires, better efficiency
48VLarge coaches, fifth wheels, serious off-gridLightest10,000W+Quarter the current of 12V — best efficiency, higher component cost

For a 24V system, two 12V batteries are wired in series. For 48V, four 12V batteries in series (or two 24V). Ensure all components — charge controller, inverter, DC loads — are rated for your chosen voltage.

AC Mains Voltage (240V / 120V)

This setting affects how AC appliance loads are labelled in the appliance table, what your inverter must output, and how shore power amperage is calculated.

SettingUsed In
240V (default)Australia, New Zealand, UK, Europe, most of Asia and Africa
120VNorth America (USA, Canada, Mexico), Japan, parts of Latin America

This does not change the wattage calculation (watts = volts × amps, and appliances are rated in watts regardless of mains voltage). It affects the shore power amperage display and inverter product labels.

Days of Autonomy

The number of consecutive cloudy/rainy days your battery bank must cover without any solar charging. 2–3 days is typical for most regions. The battery sizing calculation multiplies your daily Wh by this number.

Battery Type / Depth of Discharge (DoD)

How deeply you can safely discharge the battery before recharging:

  • Lead-Acid / AGM — 50% DoD: Discharging deeper significantly shortens lifespan.
  • LiFePO4 — 80% DoD: Standard recommendation for lithium iron phosphate.
  • Premium LiFePO4 — 90% DoD: Achievable with quality cells and a conservative charge profile, but reduces cycle life slightly.

System Derate Factor

This is a combined efficiency factor (not panel efficiency) that accounts for real-world losses: temperature derating of panels in heat, wiring resistance, MPPT conversion losses, soiling, and mismatch between panels. 80% is typical for a well-maintained system. Use 75% if you have partial shading, older wiring, or hot climates. Use 85% only for ideal conditions.

3. Adding Appliances

Add every device you plan to run. Be realistic about daily hours — this is the single biggest factor in your system size.

Appliance Type: AC vs DC

DC loads run directly from your battery (e.g. 12V/24V fridge, LED lights, fan, water pump, USB charging). These are more efficient because they don't go through the inverter.

AC loads run from your inverter (e.g. laptops, TVs, kitchen appliances, CPAP, power tools). The inverter adds ~10% loss, which is automatically accounted for in sizing.

💡 Tip: Convert as many loads as possible to native DC to reduce inverter losses and simplify your system. A 12V compressor fridge is far more efficient than a household AC fridge running off an inverter.

4. Understanding Sizing Results

Core Formulas

Battery Ah = (Daily Wh × Autonomy days) ÷ System V ÷ DoD × 1.20 buffer
Solar W = (Daily Wh ÷ Peak Sun Hrs ÷ Derate) × 1.25 buffer
Controller A (output) = Solar W ÷ System V
Inverter VA = Peak AC W × 1.25 (continuous) | Peak AC W × 2.5 (surge est.)

Battery Bank

The recommended Ah includes a 20% buffer above the calculated minimum. This accounts for battery aging, manufacturing variation, and unexpected extra loads. For a 24V or 48V system, your bank Ah stays the same — you achieve it by wiring batteries in series (voltage) and parallel (capacity).

Solar Array

The required wattage is calculated at your chosen derate factor. This is the installed array size needed, not panel nameplate efficiency. A 400W panel in shade is not 400W — hence the derate.

Panel configuration: On a 12V battery system, panels are typically wired in parallel (keeping voltage at ~18V for a 12V MPPT). On 24V/48V, you can run panels in series to raise PV voltage, which reduces current and allows thinner wire runs from the roof.

Charge Controller — Output Amps vs PV Input Voltage

The controller is sized by its output (battery charge) current in amps. This is calculated as Solar W ÷ System V.

Separately, every MPPT controller has a maximum PV input voltage rating (commonly 75V, 100V, or 150V) that is a fixed product specification, independent of battery voltage. Your panel string's open-circuit voltage (Voc) must not exceed this at the coldest expected temperature. Always verify this in the controller's datasheet.

🚫 If panel string Voc exceeds the controller's PV input rating, the controller will be permanently damaged on the first cold morning. Check the datasheet.

Inverter — Continuous vs Surge Rating

Every inverter has two ratings:

  • Continuous rating: Maximum sustained load (e.g. 2000W) — size this to cover all AC loads running simultaneously.
  • Surge/peak rating: Brief spikes the inverter can handle for 1–5 seconds (typically 2× continuous). Motors draw 2–3× their running wattage at startup — a 1500W air conditioner may draw 4000W+ at startup.

The app estimates surge at 2.5× peak AC watts. This is a conservative approximation — verify the startup current (LRA — locked rotor amps) of your specific motor-driven appliances.

Shore Power / Generator

Shore power amps shown are based on peak load at your AC mains voltage. Average draw will be significantly lower. Generators should be sized to run at 50–80% of their rated capacity for fuel efficiency and longevity. Running a generator below 30% load causes wet stacking and premature wear.

5. Component Selector

The Components tab shows built-in product recommendations sized to your calculated requirements, plus the ability to add custom products from any retailer.

Hiding Options

Click Hide on any built-in card to remove it from view. A "Restore N hidden options" button appears below each category so you can bring them back. This lets you keep only the options you're seriously considering without deleting them.

Adding Custom Products

Click + Add Custom [type] under any category. Enter the product name, spec description, price, and a URL to any online retailer (Amazon, manufacturer site, etc.). Custom products appear with a purple "Custom" badge and are included in the Shopping List.

Battery Quantity Estimate

Each battery card shows "Need ~X units" based on the product's actual Ah rating and your required bank size. A 200Ah battery needs half as many units as a 100Ah battery. This is an estimate — always verify with exact battery specs.

Shopping List

Select one product in each category and the Shopping List is generated automatically with prices and buy links. It also reminds you to budget for ancillary items: fuses, bus bars, battery monitor, wiring, and transfer switch.

6. Wiring Guide

Wire Sizing

The wire sizing table shows one-way maximum run lengths for 12V DC at 3% voltage drop. Key points:

  • Round-trip = double the distance (wire goes out and back). For a 10 ft one-way run, you need 20 ft of wire.
  • For 24V systems, distances roughly double for the same gauge and amps. For 48V, roughly quadruple.
  • Always use a voltage drop calculator for your exact run, load, and wire length.
  • When in doubt, use the next heavier gauge.
  • Use tinned marine-grade stranded copper throughout — not household electrical wire.
⚠️ All AC 240V wiring must be performed by a licensed electrician in Australia, NZ, and the UK. All AC 120V wiring in North America should follow NEC guidelines and be inspected by a qualified electrician.

Fusing

Every positive wire must be fused within 18 inches (45cm) of the battery positive terminal. This is the most critical safety rule — a short circuit in unfused wiring can cause a fire within seconds. Use ANL fuses for high-current runs and blade fuses or circuit breakers for branch circuits.

Installation Order

The app generates a step-by-step sequence based on your system. Always: (1) batteries first, (2) bus bars and main fuse, (3) charge controller battery side, (4) solar, (5) inverter, (6) AC wiring. Connect the negative battery cable last, and always disconnect it first when servicing.

8. Saving & Comparing Systems

Saving a System

Click 💾 Save System (header, sidebar, or Saved tab). Enter a name and optional notes. The saved snapshot includes all appliances, preferences, component selections, and custom products.

Note: Saves are stored in memory only and will be lost when you close or refresh the browser. Export your design notes manually if you need to keep them long-term.

Loading a System

Click Load on any saved system. This replaces the current active design with the saved appliances, preferences and selections. You are taken back to the Loads tab.

Comparing Systems

Tick the checkbox next to 2 or more saved systems, then click ⚖️ Compare. The comparison modal shows a side-by-side table of all key specifications. Green highlighting marks the higher value in each comparable row.

Useful for comparing: 12V vs 24V versions of the same build, AGM vs LiFePO4 battery sizing impact, conservative vs optimistic sun hours scenarios.

9. Accuracy & Limitations

⚠️ This tool provides planning estimates, not engineering specifications. All results should be verified by a qualified solar installer before purchase.

What the calculations do well

  • Battery sizing using correct DoD, autonomy, and voltage — with a safety buffer.
  • Solar sizing accounting for system derate (not just panel efficiency).
  • Both continuous and estimated surge ratings for inverter sizing.
  • Per-product battery and panel quantity estimates using actual product Ah/W ratings.
  • AC load accounting for inverter efficiency losses.

Known limitations

  • Peak sun hours: The app uses a single value for all days. In practice, solar is seasonal — summer may give 7 hrs, winter 2–3 hrs at the same location. Size for your worst-case season if full-timing.
  • Battery temperature: LiFePO4 capacity reduces below 0°C (32°F) and charging is prohibited below 0°C without a heated battery. This is not modelled.
  • Appliance hours: The tool assumes appliances run the same hours every day. Refrigerators, in particular, cycle on/off and actual consumption depends on ambient temperature and insulation.
  • Self-discharge: Battery self-discharge and parasitic DC loads (BMS, battery monitor, etc.) are not included in the daily Wh total.
  • Prices: Component prices are approximate and may be outdated. Always check current pricing at the retailer.

10. Frequently Asked Questions

Why does the charge controller show amps, not watts?

MPPT charge controllers are rated by their output (battery charge) current in amps. This is because the battery bank size and voltage determines the maximum charge current — not the solar array size alone. The calculation is: Solar W ÷ System V = required output amps. Separately, the controller has a maximum PV input voltage rating (e.g. 75V, 100V) which is a fixed specification — check your controller's datasheet.

Do I need a 240V inverter in Australia?

Yes. If your AC mains voltage is 240V (Australia, NZ, UK, Europe), you need a 240V inverter. Set the AC Mains Voltage setting to 240V. Most quality inverters are available in both 120V and 240V variants. Do not use a 120V North American inverter on a 240V system.

Can I mix 12V and 24V components?

No. Your entire DC system must operate at the same voltage. Your charge controller, inverter, DC loads (fridge, fans, lights), and battery bank must all be rated for the same system voltage. The only exception is a 48V inverter-charger that includes a built-in DC-DC converter for 12V accessories (some high-end units do this).

Should I choose 12V or 24V for my build?

Use 12V if your solar array is under 800W and you have minimal high-power AC loads. Move to 24V if you need more than ~800W of solar, have a large battery bank (400Ah+), or find yourself running into heavy wire gauge requirements. 48V is for serious full-time setups with air conditioning and large arrays.

Why is my inverter surge estimate so high?

The app estimates surge at 2.5× your total peak AC wattage, which can be conservative if you have multiple motor loads. In practice, not all motors start simultaneously — what matters is the single largest motor's startup current. Check the LRA (Locked Rotor Amps) spec of your air conditioner or largest compressor. Multiply by your AC voltage to get startup watts, and make sure your inverter's surge rating exceeds this.

My solar requirement seems too high — what can I reduce?

The biggest levers in order of impact:

  1. Reduce AC loads — move appliances to native DC (12V fridge instead of AC fridge, DC fans instead of AC fans).
  2. Reduce hours of use — be realistic about actual daily run time.
  3. Increase peak sun hours — if you're in a sunny region and set conservatively, adjust upward.
  4. Increase autonomy days only to what you actually need — 1 day vs 3 days tripled your battery and solar size.
  5. Use premium LiFePO4 at 90% DoD to extract more from the same battery bank.

Is my data saved between sessions?

No. The app stores everything in browser memory only. If you close or refresh the tab, your saved systems will be lost. Take screenshots or notes of your final design. Future versions may add local storage or export features.
